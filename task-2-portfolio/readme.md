Digital literacy is the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information using digital technologies — but more than that, it is the capacity to act responsibly and critically within digital environments. It goes beyond knowing how to use a device or an app. It encompasses understanding why digital decisions matter, how online actions leave lasting traces, and what it means to participate ethically in an increasingly connected world.

1. My Position: Digital Presence Benefits Students — But Only If They Go In With Eyes Open Let me state my argument clearly upfront: building a digital presence on platforms likeLinkedIn and GitHub does genuinely benefit students — but that benefit is not automatic,and the risks are more serious than most students realize when they start. The two thingsare not contradictory. You can believe that something is worth doing and simultaneouslybelieve that it is being done badly, by most people, most of the time.That is more or less the situation with student digital profiles today. The opportunity is real.
The misuse is also real. Understanding both — rather than celebrating one and ignoringthe other — is what it means to think critically about digital identity.
2. The "Equal Opportunity" Argument — Partially True, MostlyIncompleteThere is a popular narrative that digital platforms democratize opportunity. The idea is that
a student from a smaller city, with fewer connections and a less prestigious institution, can present their work online and compete on equal terms with someone from a big-citycollege. LinkedIn, GitHub, Kaggle — these platforms theoretically level the playing field.What matters is what you can show, not where you studied.
This is partly true and worth taking seriously. There are genuine examples of self-taughtdevelopers landing jobs because their GitHub showed real, working projects. There arepeople who built networks through LinkedIn that they could not have built through geography alone. The platform-as-equalizer story is not fiction.But the equalizer narrative has a significant blind spot. It assumes that all students approach these platforms with equal sophistication. In reality, students who already come from better-resourced environments — those with mentors, peers, or family members in
professional fields — are better positioned to use these platforms strategically. They know how to frame their experience. They know what recruiters look for. They know what "looks good" versus what looks like empty padding. For students without that background, the platforms can actually amplify disadvantage.
They build profiles that follow the wrong signals — listing every tool they have ever opened, filling certifications they barely completed, posting generic content — and then wonder why the platform is not working for them. The tool is neutral; the knowledge of howto use it is not equally distributed.
3. Performance Over Authenticity — The Dominant Mode If I am being honest about what I observe in how students use platforms like LinkedIn and
GitHub, the dominant mode is performance. Not necessarily dishonesty — but a relentless orientation toward how things look rather than what they mean.
This is not accidental. These platforms are designed to reward visibility. LinkedIn's algorithm pushes content that gets engagement. GitHub shows contribution graphs that reward frequent activity. Both systems create implicit incentives to do things that can be seen — to post, to push, to connect — whether or not those actions reflect genuine development.
Arjun's case is a textbook version of this. His GitHub had repositories, but the repositoriehad incomplete or copied code. His LinkedIn listed skills he had only briefly experimented with. He was not trying to deceive anyone in a calculated way. He was responding to the environment these platforms create — one where presence feels necessary, where not being on the platform feels like falling behind, and where the template for "a good profile" is
visible to everyone and therefore imitable by everyone.
4. On Misrepresentation — Is It Unethical or Just Normal? There is an argument, fairly common among students, that slight exaggeration on a digital profile is just the way things work. Everyone inflates a little. Everyone rounds up their experience. The alternative — radical honesty — would leave you looking worse than
peers who are doing the same thing. In a competitive environment, this feels like self-defense, not deception. I understand why this argument is appealing. And I do think there is a real difference between framing your experience in its best light (acceptable) and actively misrepresenting what you can do (not acceptable). But even mild profile inflation carries ethical weight. If you list a skill prominently enough that an employer expects it, and then you cannot deliver,
the consequence is a breach of trust. The "everyone does it" logic normalizes somethingthat has real costs — and those costs usually fall hardest on the person doing the inflating.
5. Digital Permanence — A Responsibility Students Underestimate This might be the single most underappreciated dimension of building a digital presence.
What you post, publish, or upload does not expire. The slightly inaccurate code youpushed to GitHub at nineteen is still there when you are twenty-four and applying for a serious engineering role. Students generally know this in the abstract — but abstract knowledge and visceral understanding are different things. Most nineteen-year-olds do not genuinely feel the weight of digital permanence because its consequences are delayed.
The case for building a digital presence, despite all the risks, is ultimately this: the digitalworld is where professional life increasingly happens. Opting out is not a neutral choice. The question is not whether to participate, but how to participate in a way that reflects
genuine development, honest representation, and awareness of the long-term record you are building.
Personal Reflection
 On Digital Identity and Ethics Writing this section forced me to look at my own LinkedIn profile with a more critical eye
than I ever had before. There are things listed there that I stand behind completely — projects I built, courses I genuinely completed and applied. But there are also a few entriesthat I added because they seemed like the right thing to include, not because they reflected
deep ability. I told myself it was normal. After thinking through this section seriously, I am less comfortable with that justification.
The part about digital permanence hit differently than I expected. I always knew, at some level, that what I posted stayed online. But I think I processed that as a social media concern — old photos, embarrassing posts — rather than as a professional one. The idea that a recruiter three years from now might look at code I pushed this semester and judge me not just on the code but on the pattern it represents changed how I think about what I
put online. Going forward, I want to treat every public-facing platform I use as a long-term document rather than a current snapshot. That means only adding things I can genuinely explain and defend, keeping repositories clean and honest, and thinking about what the overall pattern
of my digital presence communicates — not just what each individual piece says.

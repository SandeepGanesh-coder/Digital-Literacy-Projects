  Section C: Collaboration vs. Misuse
Digital Literacy Case Study | VITyarthi – NGMC
1. What Collaboration Platforms Actually Do — and What They Do Not Google Docs, shared coding environments, collaborative whiteboards — these tools have
genuinely transformed how group work functions in educational settings. Before their existence, collaboration meant finding time in the same physical room, emailing files back
and forth and losing track of versions, or physically printing and annotating documents. The friction was high enough that many group assignments ended up being done by one
person anyway, just for efficiency. Digital collaboration tools reduced that friction significantly. Groups can now work asynchronously across time zones, maintain a single living document, see each other's
edits in real time, leave comments, track changes, and iterate quickly. On purely logisticalterms, these are genuine improvements. They make working together easier.
 But "easier to work together" is not the same as "better learning outcomes." That distinction is important, and it is the tension at the heart of this section. The group assignment in
Arjun's case is a useful example. The tools worked perfectly. The document came together. The submission was clean and well-structured. And yet, when the professor
asked individual students to explain their sections, several could not do so adequately. The collaboration produced an output. It did not produce understanding.
2. Are These Tools Enhancing Learning or Enabling Shortcuts? The honest answer is: both, depending on how they are used. The same feature of a collaboration tool — the ability to see and build on what someone else has written — can
either deepen engagement or reduce it. If I read what you have written, think about it critically, add something genuinely different, and understand the whole better because of
our exchange, the tool has enhanced learning. If I skim what you have written and make minor formatting tweaks so there is a record of my contribution, the tool has enabled
avoidance. The problem is that platforms like Google Docs cannot distinguish between these twomodes. The contribution log shows activity, not quality. Accountability for the quality of
engagement is invisible in the tool itself — it has to come from somewhere else. In Arjun's case, the collaboration infrastructure was entirely adequate. The learning infrastructure
was not. Nobody asked group members to explain their reasoning during the process. Theassignment was evaluated as a group output, not as individual learning.
3. Where Is the Line Between Collaboration and Academic Dishonesty? This is genuinely a hard question. The boundary between collaboration and academic
dishonesty is inherently ambiguous at the edges — and digital tools have made the middle ground much larger than it used to be. There are clear cases on both ends. Using a shared
document to divide research tasks and then write your section based on your own reading: clearly legitimate. Copying two paragraphs from a classmate's section without modification
or understanding: clearly dishonest. The problem is the enormous grey zone between those extremes. Does the digital environment make ethical behavior harder to enforce? Yes, significantly.
The features that make collaboration convenient — simultaneous editing, easy copying, contribution logs that show presence but not effort — also make unethical behavior easier
to disguise. An instructor reviewing a Google Doc cannot reliably distinguish between a student who deeply understood the work and one who polished sentences they never truly
engaged with.
4. The Strongest Case for Digital Collaboration — And Why I Partly Disagree Here is the strongest version of the pro-collaboration argument: digital tools do not just
make group work logistically easier — they actually develop skills that matter in professional life. Real workplaces use Google Workspace, shared codebases, and
collaborative project management tools. Students who learn to work in these environments while at university are building genuine professional competencies. Moreover,
collaboration itself is intellectually valuable. Encountering a peer's approach to a problem, negotiating different interpretations, integrating multiple perspectives — these are
substantive learning experiences that individual work cannot replicate. This argument is genuinely strong. I do not think digital collaboration should be restricted or
treated with suspicion as a default. Where I diverge is on the word "genuine." The argument for digital collaboration's value depends entirely on that qualifier — and it is a
qualifier that current assessment structures largely do not enforce. The conclusion I draw is not that these tools should be restricted, but that they need to be paired with assessment
designs that make individual understanding visible: verbal explanations, reflective logs, structured peer reviews. Without them, the strongest argument for digital collaboration
becomes, in practice, a strong argument for undetectable free-riding.
Personal Reflection
 On Collaboration and My Own Group Work ExperienceI have been part of enough group assignments to recognize Arjun's situation from bothsides.
  There have been times when I was the person who carried a disproportionate shareof the work and watched others receive the same grade. And if I am being honest, there
have also been times when I contributed less than I could have — when I let someoneelse's strong draft carry a section I did not fully engage with. Neither situation felt
dramatically wrong in the moment. Both feel less defensible now.
What this section helped me articulate is why those situations felt comfortable at the time:the tools made them invisible. Nobody flagged the imbalance. The document looked like a
team effort. Grades were shared equally. The platform provided cover — not because itwas designed to, but because it was not designed to prevent it either.
Going forward, I want to hold myself to a simple test before submitting any group work:could I explain every section of this submission in detail, including parts I did not primarily
write? If the answer is no, I have not engaged enough — regardless of what thecontribution log says. That is a personal standard, not an external one. But I think it is the
right one.

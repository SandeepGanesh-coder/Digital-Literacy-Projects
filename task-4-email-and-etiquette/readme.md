Section D: Cyber Awareness and Responsibility
Digital Literacy Case Study | VITyarthi – NGMC
1. Why Awareness Is Not Enough — The Central ParadoxHere is the most uncomfortable truth in Arjun's phishing story: he already knew. He knewwhat phishing was. He had sat through a session on it. He could have, if asked at any
other moment, correctly described the warning signs of a suspicious email. And then hegot an email with all of those warning signs and submitted his login credentials anyway.
This is the central paradox of cyber security education as it currently exists. Awarenesscampaigns are largely built on the assumption that if people know about a risk, they will
avoid it. But knowing about a risk and consistently behaving safely in the presence of thatrisk are two different cognitive tasks. Arjun did not fail because he lacked information. He
failed because the emotional conditions in that moment — the excitement of a potential internship, the urgency of a deadline, the professional-looking scam — overrode the
analytical part of his mind that would have applied what he knew.
2. Why Cyber Crimes Keep Succeeding — A Structural ExplanationPhishing, fraud, and identity theft persist not because the general population is ignorant of
them, but because these crimes are designed by people who understand humanpsychology better than most cyber security awareness programs do. Attackers are notsending random emails and hoping for the best. They are conducting targeted campaigns
that exploit specific psychological triggers: urgency, authority, scarcity, and desire.The internship scam that caught Arjun used every one of these. It referenced something he
actually wanted. It claimed to come from an authority. It created scarcity and urgency.Each element was engineered to suppress careful thinking and accelerate action. Against
that kind of design, a general awareness that "phishing exists" is not particularly strongprotection. Beyond individual attacks, the digital platforms students use daily also condition
behaviors that make them more vulnerable — instant notifications train us to respond immediately, and attackers exploit the same reflexes that platforms have spent years
conditioning.
3. Who Is Responsible — The Individual, the Platform, or the
Institution?
There is a tendency in cyber security discourse to frame responsibility as almost entirelyindividual. Do not click suspicious links. Verify the sender. Use two-factor authentication.
These are correct pieces of advice. But attributing responsibility exclusively to individuals is both factually incomplete and ethically questionable. Email providers have significantly
more capacity than individual users to identify and filter fraudulent messages. When email
providers fail to filter an obvious phishing attempt and the individual who receives it clicksthrough, the responsibility is not located entirely in the individual's click.
Institutions — universities, in particular — also carry responsibility. If a student has learnedthat phishing exists but has never practiced identifying it in realistic conditions, the
institution has provided information without developing the skill that the information issupposed to support. That is an educational failure, not just an individual one.
4. Scenario Analysis — Negligence, Education Failure, or Systemic
Problem?  If a student falls victim to an online scam, how should we characterize what went wrong?
The default framing tends toward "the student should have known better." This issimultaneously true, incomplete, and unhelpful. It is true in the sense that better habits and
more skepticism would have helped. It is incomplete because individual behavior is shaped by platform design, prior conditioning, and emotional context — none of which the student
is entirely responsible for. A more useful framing: the student's vulnerability was enabled by a combination of factors
— a gap between knowing and doing, a moment of emotional override, a platform that did not catch a detectable threat, and an educational environment that provided information
without building habitual skepticism. Addressing only one — typically by lecturing students about being more careful — is not a serious response to the problem.
5. Are Current Prevention Measures Sufficient?
No. The reason they are insufficient is not that they are wrong — most standard advice is correct and genuinely helpful. The reason they are insufficient is that they address the
wrong level of the problem. Current prevention measures are largely informational and procedural. What they rarely do is build the behavioral habit of skepticism that would make
safe behavior automatic rather than effortful. Habits require repetition and reinforcement in
conditions that resemble the real situations in which they need to operate. A one-hour workshop does not build that kind of habit.Cyber safety education is still largely designed as if information transfer is the goal. The
goal should be behavioral change — and that requires a different approach entirely. Arjun's
case is a clear demonstration. He had the information. The behavior did not follow
automatically. That gap is where the real work of cyber education needs to happen.
Personal Reflection
 On Cyber Awareness and My Own BehaviorReading about Arjun clicking on that link without pausing was uncomfortable because it felt
entirely plausible for me. I have received suspicious emails that I dismissed immediately —and I have also received suspicious emails that I almost did not dismiss, because the
timing was right and the offer looked real. The difference between those two situations wasnot knowledge. It was context and emotional state. That is a humbling thing to admit.
I have sat through cyber security sessions. I know what phishing looks like in a controlled,educational setting, where the warning signs are pointed out and the outcome is already
known. But a real phishing email does not arrive in that calm, analytical frame. It arriveswhen you are busy, hopeful, or distracted. The gap between recognizing phishing in a
classroom exercise and recognizing it in the moment it targets something you actually want— that gap is real, and I now take it much more seriously than I did before.One specific habit I am committing to after this: any email that involves an opportunity and
creates urgency in the same message gets a mandatory delay. I will not respond within thefirst hour, no matter how exciting it looks. If it is real, it will survive an hour of verification. If
it evaporates under scrutiny, it was never real. That small rule would have protected Arjun. I Think it will protect me too.
